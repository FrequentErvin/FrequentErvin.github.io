A life lesson & cool problems

October 25, 2021 by Ervin

While going through the olympiad system in my country we used to learn almost no theory and that was especially a thing in combinatorics. I don't think that we had a specific lesson in combinatorial theory at all. I think it's a good thing to know "names" of some techniques of solving problems, even though they can't be labbeled as nicely as in geometry. For example, I wanted to dedicate this section to a specific problem. Not so long ago this problem appeared on our TST (here in Bosnia):

BIHMO 2020 D1 P1

Integers are written in the vertices of a regular $n$-gon, so that the differences between any two numbers in adjacent vertices is $0$ or $1$. These vertices split the circumcircle of the $n$-gon into $n$ equal arcs. In the midpoints of each arc, the arithmetic mean of the numbers that form that arc is written. Determine all n such that with certainty one can conclude that there are two diametrically opposite points on this circle which have equal value.

This here is the case for $n=5$. Notice the diametrically opposite points with value $3$.

After the contest had ended:

How was this problem 1? I don't know a single person who solved it in less than 2 hours.

Before I start explaining the solution of the above problem, let me show you some other problems.

  1. Prove that all roots of the polynomial $P(x)=x(x-2)(x-4)(x-6)+(x-1)(x-3)(x-5)(x-7)$ are real.
  2. (Serbia MO 2014) Call a positive integer $n$ crazy iff there exist positive integers $a,b>1$ such that $n=a^b+b$. Prove that there exist $2014$ consecutive positive integers such that there are exactly $2012$ crazy numbers among them.
  3. (USAMO 2005) Suppose $2n$ points are given in the plane, no three of which are collinear. Suppose $n$ of them are colored blue and the other $n$ are colored red. A line in the plane is called a balancing line if it passes through one blue and one red point and, for each side of the line, the number of blue points on that side is exactly equal to the number of red points on the same side. Prove that there exist at least two balancing lines.

And their respective "outline" solutions:

Problem 1
Look at the polynomials at the inputs $x=0,1,2,3,4,5,6,7$. Pay particular attention to the sign of the value you get. What can you conclude with the information that $P(0) > 0$ and $P(1) < 0 $?
Problem 2
Notice that the first $2012$ numbers aren't all crazy and then note that by shifting an interval of $2014$ consecutive numbers we change the number of crazy numbers in the interval by no more than 1 thus we can hit all values.
Problem 3
Playing around with the line, it makes sense to move it.

The point

All three of these problems share the same vibe. They all rely on some sort of continuity. We rigorously define this later. I present the solution of the introductory problem in two steps:

Solution:

  • The antipode of a vertex in a regular $n$-gon is either a midarc (when $n$ is odd) or a vertex (when $n$ is even). Similar holds for the midarcs (vertex - midarc respectively)
  • Notice that for $n=2k+1$ (odd) we can just place the numbers $0,1,2 \ldots k-1,k,k,k-1, \ldots ,1,0$ as the vertices. Now there can't be any antipodal points with the same value since one of them is a midarc (which is not an integer) and one of them is a vertex (which is an integer). Therefore, $n$ isn't odd.

    • The only integer value is the one $4$, but it's antipode is $0$ so it's good.

  • For $n=2k$ we prove there always must exist two antipodal points of equal value. Place a pencil in the middle such that it points to the bigger number always. Start rotating it clockwise. At one point the pencil must flip since if it doesn't that would mean it came back to the beginning position but reversed.

    • When the pencil flips, the midarc between those two points have equal value, hence we're done.
