Projectile polynomials

Mar 17, 2022

A brief overview of projectile motion:

"Flip a coin until you get tails. This attack does $30$ damage times the number of heads."

Assumptions I'll make because of clarity: the body is launched inside Earth's gravitational field, there is no air resistance, disregard curvature of the Earth. Up is positive, down is negative (necessary for velocities).
Let the object be flung into the air at an angle $\theta$ with initial speed $v$. Gravity acts on our body perpendicularly to the surface of the Earth so our object will be affected in the vertical $y$ direction. Let our vertical velocity at time $t$ be $v_{y}^{(t)}$ and let our horizontal velocity be $v_{x}^{(t)}$. Notice that since nothing exerts force on our body in the $x$ direction our horizontal velocity stays the same so $v_{x}^{(t)} = v_{x}^{(0)}$ for all $t$. Our vertical velocity is trickier since we have to account for the deacceleration $g=9.8 \ldots$ in the vertical direction, thus our vertical velocity at time $t$ is $v_{y}^{(t)} = v_{y}^{(0)}\cdot t - g\cdot t$. At some point, our $v_y$ will become zero and our object will again fall down to the Earth (assume no escape velocity and $g$ is constant at all distances). Our displacement in the $x$ direction is just equal to $s_{x}^{(t)} = v_{x}^{(t)} * t$. Our $y$ displacement is $s_{y}^{(t)} = v_{y}^{(0)} \cdot t - \frac{g\cdot t^2}{2}$.
